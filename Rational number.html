Lesson: Equivalent & Comparing Fractions

Two bakers, two cakes, and one big question: does cutting something into more pieces ever change how much you actually have?

CCSS 4.NF.A.1  &  4.NF.A.2

You already know that a fraction like 12 means one of two equal parts of a whole. But the same amount can be written many different ways — and knowing when two fractions are secretly the same amount (or which one is bigger) is one of the most useful skills in mathematics, from splitting a pizza fairly to reading a recipe to understanding a scale drawing.

Imagine two friends, Meera and Arjun, each baking an identical rectangular cake for a class party.

Arjun claims he ate more cake than Meera, "because 2 is bigger than 1, and 4 is bigger than 2!" Meera isn't so sure. Before we settle their argument, let's build the tools to answer it properly.

1. What Makes Fractions Equivalent

Look at what happens if we re-cut Meera's cake without eating any more of it — just slicing every existing piece into smaller, equal pieces.

Number of equal pieces the cake is cut into246812
Pieces Meera has eaten12346
Fraction of the cake1/22/43/64/86/12
Key Insight
Every column in that table describes the exact same amount of cake. Only the number and size of the pieces changed — never the amount eaten. This is the whole idea behind equivalent fractions.
Definition 1.1 · Equivalent Fractions
Two fractions are equivalent if they name the same amount of the same whole. If you multiply (or divide) both the numerator and denominator of a fraction by the same nonzero number, you get an equivalent fraction:

ab  =  a × nb × n    for any whole number n ≠ 0

Example 1 · Generating Equivalent Fractions

Find three fractions equivalent to 34.

Multiply top and bottom by the same number each time:

3/4 × 2/2 = 6/8   |   3/4 × 3/3 = 9/12   |   3/4 × 4/4 = 12/16

Each of 6/8, 9/12, and 12/16 shades the exact same amount of a same-sized whole as 3/4 — they only look different because the whole is sliced more finely.

Watch out for this trap
Adding the same number to the numerator and denominator does not create an equivalent fraction — only multiplying (or dividing) both by the same number does. For example, 1/2 is not equivalent to 2/3 (add 1 to top and bottom), even though it feels like a similar move. Try this yourself with the "Show the trap" button in the tool below.

2. Comparing Fractions

Now back to Meera and Arjun. To settle their argument, we need a fair way to compare 1/2 and 2/4 — fractions with different denominators.

Definition 1.2 · Comparing Fractions
To compare two fractions fairly, rewrite them so they share a common whole. Two common strategies:
  • Common denominator: rewrite both fractions with the same denominator, then compare numerators directly.
  • Benchmark fractions: compare each fraction to a familiar landmark such as 1/2 or 1 to reason about size without recomputing everything.
Example 2 · Settling the Cake Argument

Compare 1/2 and 2/4 using a common denominator.

The denominators 2 and 4 share a common multiple of 4. Rewrite 1/2 as 2/4 (multiply top and bottom by 2). Now both fractions read 2/4 and 2/4 — they are equal. Meera and Arjun ate exactly the same amount of cake; Arjun's slices were just smaller and more numerous.

Example 3 · Using a Benchmark

Which is greater: 3/5 or 5/8?

3/5 is a little more than 1/2 (since 2.5/5 would be exactly 1/2). 5/8 is also a little more than 1/2 (since 4/8 would be exactly 1/2). Both are "just past halfway," so the benchmark alone isn't decisive here — we need a common denominator. Using 40: 3/5 = 24/40 and 5/8 = 25/40. Since 25 > 24, 5/8 is slightly greater than 3/5.

Key Insight
A bigger denominator does not mean a bigger fraction — it means the whole is cut into more, smaller pieces. Always ask: same whole, same size pieces? Only then can numerators be compared directly.

Key Takeaways
  • Equivalent fractions describe the same amount of the same whole, written with different-sized pieces.
  • Multiplying (or dividing) the numerator and denominator by the same nonzero number produces an equivalent fraction. Adding the same number to both does not.
  • To compare fractions fairly, rewrite them with a common denominator, or reason using a benchmark like 1/2 or 1.
  • A larger denominator means smaller pieces, not automatically a larger fraction — the numerator and denominator must be judged together, against the same whole.

Assignment: The Fraction Detective

6 questions Suggested time: 40–50 minutes Answer in full sentences & show your reasoning

These questions climb from simply recalling the idea of equivalence up to designing your own fraction puzzle. Explain your thinking in words as well as numbers — a correct symbol with no reasoning earns only partial credit.

Question 1Remember

Your younger cousin asks, "So does 2/4 just mean 2 things and 4 things?"

In your own words, explain what the numerator and denominator of a fraction each represent, and explain why 2/4 and 1/2 describe the same amount even though the numbers look different. Use the cake example from the lesson if it helps.

Question 2Understand

A tailor has a ribbon that is exactly 3/5 of a meter long.

Write three fractions equivalent to 3/5 by multiplying the numerator and denominator by the same number each time. Then explain, in a sentence, why multiplying both parts by the same number never changes the actual length of ribbon — even though the numbers change.

Question 3Apply

A community garden has two identical rectangular plots.

Plot A has 5/6 of its area planted with tomatoes. Plot B has 7/9 of its area planted with tomatoes. Using a common denominator, determine which plot has more of its area planted, and show every step of your work. Then state your answer as a full sentence a gardener could understand.

Question 4Analyze

Two students are comparing 4/9 and 5/8 and disagree on their method.

Student A says: "5/8 is bigger because 5 > 4." Student B says: "We can't tell without a common denominator." Analyze both arguments. Identify exactly which part of Student A's reasoning breaks down, find the correct comparison using a common denominator or benchmark, and explain what conceptual mistake Student A is making about how fractions work.

Question 5Evaluate

A classmate writes on a test: "Since 6/8 has bigger numbers than 3/4, 6/8 must be a larger fraction."

Evaluate this claim. Is it true, partially true, or false? Support your judgment with a specific calculation or visual argument, and explain the deeper misunderstanding about fractions that leads students to make this kind of claim.

Question 6Create

You are writing a puzzle for next year's class.

Design your own real-world scenario (something other than cake, ribbon, or a garden) that involves exactly one pair of equivalent fractions and one pair of fractions that look similar but are not equivalent. Write out both fraction pairs, explain — using Definition 1.1 — why the first pair is equivalent, and explain why the second pair is not, even though it might look like it should be at first glance.

Question 13 pts

Which fraction is equivalent to 2/3?

8/12 = 2/3 because both the numerator and denominator of 2/3 were multiplied by 4.
Question 23 pts

A student writes 3/5 = 5/7 by adding 2 to the numerator and denominator. What is wrong with this?

Equivalent fractions come from multiplying or dividing both parts by the same nonzero number — not from adding the same amount to both.
Question 33 pts

Which comparison is correct?

Using twelfths: 7/12 stays 7/12, and 2/3 = 8/12. Since 7 < 8, 7/12 < 2/3.
Question 43 pts

Why can two fractions with different denominators still represent the same amount?

Cutting the same whole into more, smaller equal pieces (and shading proportionally more of them) never changes the total amount represented.
Question 53 pts

Which statement about benchmark fractions is true?

If one fraction is clearly above 1/2 and the other clearly below, the benchmark alone settles the comparison — no common denominator needed.
